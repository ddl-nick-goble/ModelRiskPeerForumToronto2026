FRICTION

If more models leads to more pain, your governance isn’t working.

When governance works, you don’t notice it.
When you’re feeling friction, that’s the governance failing.

Six months to put a model into production is death by a thousand paper cuts.

Hand-typing test evidence and model documentation isn’t the cost of governance. It means our tools haven’t caught up to what a model actually is yet.

WHAT’S AN EPICYCLE?

Roadblocks come stair-stepped.

You add a committee.

Then a validation exercise on top.

Then a sub-policy bolted onto that.

There’s a word for this.
Epicycles.

You don’t fix an epicycle by adding another epicycle. You throw the diagram out and start over.

Find the ground truth.

EPICYCLES

We have 80 epicycles in MRM. Some are visible.

REGULATORY
  • OSFI E-23 (2017) → E-23 revised (2027) mapping workstream
  • AIDA / Bill C-27 crosswalk addendum
  • CAPSA / FSRA pension overlay
  • Quarterly attestation memo to the CRO
INTERNAL POLICY
  • Standalone “Responsible AI” policy
  • GenAI working group, weekly
  • EUC carve-out for spreadsheets
  • Separate vendor model policy
INVENTORY
  • Second “AI inventory,” reconciled by hand
  • LLM / prompt registry
  • Vendor model attestation tracker
  • Shadow inventory in SharePoint
EVIDENCE & APPROVAL
  • GenAI sub-committee of the MRMC
  • Bias & fairness appendix on every report
  • Hallucination test pack (revised quarterly)
  • SHAP-or-die explainability section

Every single one of these solves a real problem. But the fact that there are eighty of them is a red flag. Nobody ever went back and rewrote the definition of “model.”

TIMELINE

Only following lagged regulations is an epicycle.

Average lag: three to five years. Each framework solved the previous crisis; each gap it left became another circle to add to the diagram.

Each rule tried to fix the last breakdown. None of them saw the next one coming.

What fixing it actually looks like.

It gets worse before it gets better. Then the velocity compounds.

WHAT TO DO ABOUT IT

Define the model.

One definition. A shared language for model risk. OSFI’s revised E-23 already calls AI/ML a model, but all governance hasn’t caught up yet.

A model is simply a function.

Governance has to cover the function, not just the formula.

A hedging program is a series of functions.

AI is just a series of functions.

STEP TWO

Defining trust.

01 Risk tells us what’s at stake. Trust tells us if that’s reliable.
02 Gaps in trust are covered through additional oversight and governance.
03 This helps avoid human-in-the-loop “theater.”
RISK / TRUST

The same question, asked backwards.

Risk is what you stand to lose if the model is wrong. Trust is how much you actually know about what it’s going to do. Same world, measured from opposite ends.

TRUST · HOW MUCH IS ENOUGH

How much trust is enough?

Coverage and fidelity tell you how much trust you have. The stakes determine how much you need. Where trust falls short, the gap is closed by oversight — keeping a human in the loop.

Same trust, different stakes. When trust clears the bar, you automate. When it doesn’t, the gap is exactly where a person stays in the loop — and where OSFI E-23 and the EU AI Act set the bar.

THE FULL PICTURE

Governance starts where trust stops.

Every model has four layers: the inputs it consumes, the environment it runs in, the model itself, and the outputs it produces. Score trust across all four and governance stops being a judgment call. It goes exactly where the score is thin.

Each system creates its own epicycles.

A single source of truth simplifies everything.
